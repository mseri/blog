The commoditization of AI—and its rapidly improving ability to solve, explain, and even generate mathematical problems—is increasingly getting in the way of our teaching, and even more so, disrupting our usual assessment methods.
For instance, many courses have seen an enormous increase of nearly perfect and overly elaborate solutions, whose quality is rarely reflected in the final exams. Discussion and participation seem decreasing, favouring the faster (and more impersonal) LLMs used as tutor replacements. While research and experience indicate that collaboration and active struggle are the key for learning.

While this is surely an overgeneralization, those issues have become so apparent that have been forcing us to question how we teach and assess.
After numerous discussions (and requests from staff members) at our Programme Committee, we finally decided to sit down with students and colleagues and start looking for clarity. Clearly we could not solve this in one short meeting, but having an open dialogue was extremely useful, and allowed us both to get some new ideas and to start understanding what we each think and hope for. This blog post is a summary of my impressions from the meeting.

## Rethinking ourselves

To engage in this discussion, it's essential to clarify two fundamental questions: _What is our main objective?_ and _What should students learn from our degree program?_ This comes even before we can ask how do we ensure they actually learn it.

While the Teaching and Exam regulations provide the institutional point of view on the first two questions, and the boundaries on how we can evaluate the latter, our personal answers may not be as obvious as they seem. 

From my perspective, the goal of a mathematics degree isn't just to teach mathematics. In fact, I think that our primary aim is to help our students develop analytical and critical thinking, tolerance for frustration, and collaboration skills. Mathematical knowledge becomes a means to an end in this sense.

This framing may raise eyebrows; after all, not everyone will share this point of view. That's precisely why open discussions like the one we had can be so valuable: they help us align on what we're truly trying to achieve.
If we're not even asking the same questions, how can we expect to agree on the answers?

In the meantime, the problem remains. AI has gotten in the way of our lectures, so what should we do?

The debate over whether to allow, restrict, or ban AI in education is far from settled. Research is ongoing and while the few results we have seem to hint at a detrimental effect of the technology on learning, they are still preliminary and more time is needed both in terms of understanding and developing the necessary pedagogical updates.

If top mathematicians are using these tools, why shouldn't students?

To me, the answer isn't as simple as it seems. Two concerns immediately stand out. First, access. Tools like Google Aletheia, Google DeepThink, and ChatGPT Pro (which some of our students is using!) are either restricted or very expensive (they are only available in the 200€/month plans). This is all but democratizing mathematics education.

Second, their expertise makes a huge difference. Experts can critically assess the validity of the outputs, spot mistakes, steer the conversation in useful directions. This is hardly doable while one is still learning the subject. If we let them take these shortcuts too soon, we are short-circuiting the very skills we are supposed to be cultivating: thinking, perseverance, and frustration tolerance.

## New thoughts, old problems

How much time do students actually spend wrestling with exercises? This was a question I had not considered. I am sure there are students that will engage with the material for days, flipping through their notes, discussing with peers, and testing ideas until they crack the problem. Somewhat like many us before answers started to appear in Google.

But many will not. With AI, they can get a hint, or more often a verbose, half-correct solution, in seconds. It is convenient, it does not judge, but it completely bypasses the struggle, the collaboration, and the time needed to let ideas sink in properly.

Someone suggested to set expectations for students and explicitly state that they are expected to spend X hours per exercise, say 4-8 hours. I'm a bit wary of picking some specific number, everyone works differently and goes at a different pace, but I find the principle quite interesting. Learning takes time: you need to sleep on it, at least once, and have your chances to get stuck and let your brain work on it while you do something else. By telling students when to pause and seek help, and how long to try before giving up, we may help setting the expectations. Right now we know that for many of them the time between getting stuck and seeking help (use to be google it, now it is asking an LLM) is a matter of tens of minutes. Way too short.

Interestingly, from a mix of conversations and relatively large internal survey, it is clear that newer cohorts are becoming more aware of LLM's limitations. Many have been experimenting with it, or thrown into it since now it is practically being attached to anything, and saw first hand how it can mislead and go off the rail. From their answers, many can see how it can shallow their understanding. But there's still a significant pressure to optimize for grades, where AI becomes a great help to cut corners.

But is any of this something new? Many of the problems that we raised about participation, about overfocus on the grade, about reduction of frustration, are not new. Having a (possibly bad) free personal tutor, that is always awake, responds on the spot and does not judge you (in fact, it always praises you) is exponentially speeding up a problem that has been there since a long time. The explosion of LLMs is simply making the problem of how to motivate our students more evident.

And this may be a good thing, offering an opportunity where also the more sceptical of us can see the problem more clearly and start looking for solutions.

Some of us have responded by removing or ungrading exercises, but is that the answer? If other courses still offer graded homework, for instance, students will just shift their focus, and engagement will drop. We see this happening already, and students mention it themselves in our course evaluations.

## Some interesting ideas: working or work-in-progress

During the meeting some interesting ideas were brought forward and I think is worth mentioning them here, if anything as a starting point or food for thought for others that are having similar discussions.

* Replace graded homework with peer-review sessions or optional TA feedback. One needs to ensure that student actively engage with the process, which incurs in the risk discussed earlier. In the case of peer-review sessions, feedback should checked so that students can confidently rely on it, but done well it can become a very lively and insightful activity. Extra points for active participation could provide some extrinsic motivation.
* Encourage students, for example by giving them points, to present their solutions at the blackboard during tutorials. This seems to be going quite well in the few courses that tried.
* Have graded quizzes at the end of tutorials, using one exercise from previous tutorial. This seems to also work quite well.
* Let students self-grade their work. Extrinsic motivation for active participation could be done, e.g., as follows: if the average of their evaluations is close enough to the final exam grade, they get a bonus point.

The common thread in all these seems to be using low-stakes, high-value activities that push engagement over grade-chasing. To add to the list, there are some interesting ideas [in the Grading for Growth blog](https://gradingforgrowth.com/p/small-changes-to-handle-ai).

Many students also praised setting up WhatsApp groups with their tutorial groups and the TA. We need to be careful of not overwoeking the TAsin this case, but experience shows that, more often than not, questions in these groups are resolved among the students themselves before the TA even chimes in.

## What's the aim again?

After all the discussion, a question came naturally into shape. To know what activities make more sense, we need to first clarify our target.
Who are we teaching for?

Are we only trying to push the top students to excel? Do we want to keep the majority engaged without losing those who want more? Do we accept that some will always tune out? While I have the impression we have common understanding, I am not sure we are all on the same page on this yet. And this is why this type of discussions is useful and worth its time. It may not yet give the answer, but in addition to many interesting ideas, it can lay the ground to find a common understanding and a new way forward.

In the end, there still seems to be a tension between maximising the impact and optimizing for need. From both sides. Among students and staff we seek ways to save time and maximise the class' grade, we both have a large time pressure and often good solutions to our needs require a lot of time. Maybe this is were the next dialogue should start.
